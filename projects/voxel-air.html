Voxel Air

A 3D voxel editor you control with your hands in front of your webcam. It assumes hands, not a mouse and a manual.

Live · runs in this browser

Raise a hand at your webcam and move a voxel — right here.

Loads the real app only when you ask. Prefer a new tab? Open Voxel Air ↗

The problem

3D modelling tools assume a mouse and a manual. This one assumes hands.

Every 3D editor expects a mouse, a menu, and a manual to learn them. Voxel Air removes that convention: a stranger opens the page, raises a hand at the webcam, and starts building — no install, no account, no manual.

The constraint

It had to stay free to run, private by default, and deployable as static files — while working on whatever webcam a stranger happens to have. No controlled lighting. No known hardware.

The approach I rejected

Stream the webcam frames to a server and run hand-tracking there. It’s the easier build — heavier models, compute you control — but it breaks all three constraints at once: a backend to pay for and keep alive, a stranger’s camera frames leaving their machine, and the end of one-click static deploy.

The approach I took

Do all hand-tracking client-side with MediaPipe, render the voxels with Three.js and WebGL, in vanilla JavaScript. Fully static — zero backend, private by default, instant deploy.

The whole cost of that choice lands on gesture detection: it has to stay stable on unknown cameras, and that constraint drove most of the engineering.

The result

Deployed and in use. Getting feedback from developers internationally through GitHub — which is also where the gesture-stability edge cases keep coming from.

What I’d do differently

Lead with the demo from day one — a six-second clip of a hand moving a voxel says more than any paragraph on this page. And invest earlier in gesture robustness across cheap and unusual webcams, since that’s where every real-world bug has come from.
